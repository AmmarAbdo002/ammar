CH.1: introduction



Definition of energy: capacity of body to do work (potential of causing changes).
Energy categories (kinetic - potential)
- Forms of energy
Kinetic: Thermal - Mechanical - Electrical - Magnetic.
Potential: Chemical - Elastic - Nuclear - Gravitational.
Other: Sound - Light
- Energy types
Primary: supply of net energy, limited (coal - natural gas - oil - nuclear)
Secondary: no net energy (sun - Wind - Water)
Electrical: charges moving through wire.
Radiant: Electromagnetic energy travels in space like solar energy (Radio - microwave - infrared - visible light - ultraviolet - X rays - gamma)
Thermal (heat): internal energy in substance.
Geothermal
- Motion (F=ma): movement of substance & objects.
- Potential: energy of position.
- Chemical: stored in the bonds of atoms and molecules (biomass - petroleum - natural gas).
- Stored mechanical energy: (springs - bow- stretched rubber bands نبلة).
- Nuclear: energy in nucleus of an atom (fission, split of uranium nuclei).
- Gravitational: as hydropower or a rock resting at the top of the hill (gravitational potential energy).


Ch.2: batteries



- Battery: electrochemical cells (battery unit) converts chemical energy to electric.
- Primary cell: is non-rechargeable (energy stored inherently in the chemical substance)
- Secondary cell: is rechargeable (energy is induced in the electrochemical substance by an external source)

- Battery Type:
1- Automotive SLI portable batteries: starting, lighting, ignition in internal combustion engine vehicles.
2- Vehicle traction batteries (12V to 24V): in huge trucks, fork lift trucks, airport tractors.
3- Stationary batteries & standby power system: used in essential equipment, alarm systems, UPS (uninterruptible power system).

- Non-rechargeable Batteries
1- Alkaline: Zn + MnO₂ + KOH (electrolyte).
Advantages: more cycle life - small - high efficiency - low internal R - low leakage.
Disadvantage: cost is a bit high.

2- Coin Cell: alkaline, lithium, silver oxide. Steady stable voltage & small sizes.
Advantages: light weight - small size - high density - low cost - high nominal voltage (up to 3V) - long shelf life.
Disadvantages: needs a holder - low current draw capability.

- Rechargeable Batteries
1- Lead-acid.
2- Ni-Cd: AA - AAA - C - rectangular shapes.
3- Ni-MH (Nickel metal Hydride): preferable than Ni-Cad (lower environmental impact).
4- Li-ion: made of lithium, latest in rechargeable technology.

- Chemical changes in battery:
1- Discharging:
Fully charged (anode+ PbO2 & cathode- Pb).
Current is sent from cathode to anode through the electrolyte.
Voltage decreased and gives energy.
2- Charging:
During charging (anode PbO2 & cathode Pb).
Rise in voltage
Energy is absorbed by the cell

- Internal resistance:
1- Secondary cell has internal R (some voltage is dropped or lost through it).
2- Increases with the size of the plates.
3- Interlacing reduces internal R & increases capacity.

- Capacitance= I * t (Ah)

- Efficiency
1- Ah efficiency=Ah discharge / Ah charge (قانون)
2- Wh efficiency= Ah efficiency X (volts on discharge/ volts on charge) (قانون)
Efficiency of lead-acid is 90-95%

(Wh efficiency < Ah efficiency) always

- Why efficiency decrease with time?
1- Self discharge of plates due to local reactions
2- Leakage due to faulty insulation

- Battery rating
1- Ah capacity = I * t
2- Zero cranking power (zero degree performance): large batteries are tested at 300 A, 18 C or 0 F

- Full charged cell:
1- Gassing.
2- Specific gravity of the electrolyte: measured by hydrometer.
3- color: deep chocolate brown.

- Charging systems:
1- DC constant voltage source (قانون ورسمة دائرة).
I = (V-Eb)/R (3 مسائل)
(R includes internal resistance).

2- AC constant voltage source: transformer + 4 diodes bridge (rectifier) (رسمة).
Charging voltage is constant with the help of variable R.
3- AC constant current source: transformer + 4 diodes bridge (rectifier) (رسمة).

Charging current is constant with the help of variable R.


Ch.3: steam power station



- Elements of energy system (رسمة):
1- Generation: (in power station)
2- Transmission: at high voltage (over 66kV) to load centers using transmission lines, cables, transformers.
3- Distribution: to individual consumers

- Energy generating system consists of:

1- Prime mover: energy source (fuel, steam, wind...) to rotating mechanical energy.
EX:
= Steam turbines (thermal & nuclear stations)
= Gas turbines (gas stations)
= Internal combustion (diesel stations)
= Hydraulic turbines (hydraulic stations)

2- Alternator (AC generator): coupled to a steam turbine, rotating mechanical energy to electrical.

3- Transformers:
= step-up: 22kV to 500kV.
= station transformers: used for general services (lighting) in the power station.
= step-down: 500kV to 66kV

4- Dispatcher: system to collect electricity from different interconnected power stations (in the unified power grid). Then distribute it to the power system network.


- Main sources of electrical powers:
1- Fossil fuels (coal, mazout, natural gases)
2- Hydraulic power: run-of-river, reservoir medium-head hydroelectric power plants, tides from oceans.
3- Nuclear fuels.
4- Renewable energy: (solar, wind, geothermal, energy from ocean).

- Thermal power plants: most common, chemical energy in fossil fuels to rotating mechanical energy by (steam & gas & diesel turbines)

- Steam power stations: most economic
1- Thermal (coal fired) power plants.
2- Steam (mazout, natural gas fired) power plants.

- Thermal power plants consists of:
1- Fuel circuit.
2- Water feeding and hot steam circuit.
3- Cooling water circuit.

- Steam power stations (رسمة مهمة):
Operation:
1- Heat from burning coal converts water into steam in boiler.
2- Steam expanded in steam turbine (prime mover) to drive alternator to produce electrical energy.
3- Steam is condensed and fed again to boiler by pumps.
.
1- boiler: where coal is burnt, heat converts water into steam.
2- Super heater: where steam (out from boiler) is dried and super heated by the gases to increase efficiency.
3- Economizer: Heats the water (between condenser & boiler) by flue gases to increase efficiency.
4- Air preheater: where input atmosphere air is heated by flue gases (from economizer) before boiler to increase efficiency.

- Site selection factors of thermal power station:
1. Supply of fuel: near the coal mines (transportation cost is minimum).
2. Availability of water: near to river.
3. Transportation facilities: transportation of material and machinery.
4. Cost and type of land: where the land is cheap.
5. Nearness to load centers: to reduce transmission cost.
6. Distance from populated area: huge amount of coal is burnt (pollution).

- Advantages of thermal power station:
1. Fuel "coal" is cheap.
2. Less initial cost.
3. Can be installed at any place irrespective of the existence of coal.
4. It required less space (compared to the hydroelectric station).
5. The cost of generation is lesser than diesel power station.

- Disadvantages:
1. Pollutes the atmosphere.
2. costlier in running cost as compared to hydroelectric plant.

Ch.4: Diesel power plant



- Diesel generator = diesel engine + electric generator
Chemical energy of diesel oil ➡ (engine) ➡ rotating mechanical energy ➡ (electric generator, alternator) ➡ electrical energy
- Diesel power plants: used if the place has no connection to a power grid or for Emergency.
- Components vof diesel plants:
1. Diesel engine.
2. Fueling system.
3. Air inlet (cooling) system.
4. Lubricating oil system.

-Diesel engine
1. used as a prime mover.
2. May be a 2-stroke engine or 4-stroke engine.
3. The working principle of engine:
A. Compress the air to raise temperature.
B. Burn the diesel inside the engine.
C. The combustion produces fluid at high temperature and high pressure.
D. Heat energy is converted to mechanical energy.
4- The cycle of the strokes in diesel engine (intake, compression, expansion, exhaust).


- Fuel supply system: a storage tank of oil.
- Strainer: removes impurities of the oil.
- Air intake system (pipes and filters): removes dust particles from the air to supply fresh air to the engine for combustion.
- Exhaust system: removes the exhaust gas from the engine.
- Cooling system: maintains overall temperature at an agreeable level.
Requires a water source, water pump to circulate Water through a cooling system.
- Lubricating system: gives lubrication to the moving parts of engine.
Consists of lubrication oil, pump, filter, oil cooler.

- A two-stroke engine will develop twice the power of a four-stroke engine.
-The generator, coupled with the engine, converts mechanical energy into electrical.

- Advantages:
1. Simple design.
2. Low space.
3. Quickly start.
4. Low cost and higher thermal Efficiency compared to steam power Plant.
5. Small cooling water system.

- Disadvantages:
1. High running cost.
2. Used for small power.
3. High maintenance cost.
4. Doesn't work for long periods.

Ch.5: Gas power plant



Ch.5: Gas power plant (461 words)
- A gas turbine converts gas or liquid fuels to mechanical energy driving a generator that produces electrical energy.
- Gas turbines burn fuels such as oil, natural gas.

- Three main parts: (Air compressor - Combustion chamber - Turbine).

- Working principle gas station
1. Air is compressed to high pressure.
2. Then fuel and compressed air are mixed in a combustion chamber and ignited.
3. Hot gases are given off, which spins the turbine wheels.

- Basic components
1. Air compressor: Supplies high pressure air for combustion
2. Combustion chamber:
Fuel pumped in and ignited to burn with Compressed air.
Spark plugs used to ignite fuel.
3. Turbine:
Hot gases converted to work.
Drives compressor & external load.

- Gas turbine: Consists of one or more stages designed to develop rotational energy.
Uses sets of nozzles & blades.

- Applications of gas turbine:
= Drive pumps, compressors and high-speed cars.
= Aircraft and ships.
= power generation (used for peak load and as stand-by unit).

- Open cycle gas turbine:
Fresh air ➡ (Compressor) ➡ Air & fuel ➡ (Combustion chamber) ➡ (Turbine) ➡ exhaust gases.
Some of the output power is used to drive the compressor.
The exhaust gases are thrown out (not re-circulated), so (open cycle).

- Closed cycle gas turbine :
(Compressor) ➡ heat in ➡ (Heat exchanger) ➡ (Turbine) ➡ (Heat exchanger) ➡ heat out ➡ REPEAT.
The combustion process is replaced by a constant-pressure heat addition.
The exhaust process is replaced by a constant pressure heat rejection.

-Advantages of gas turbine power plant:
1. Storage of fuel requires less area and handling is easy.
2. The cost of maintenance is less.
3. Simple in construction. There is no need for boiler, condenser and other accessories as in the case of steam power plants.
4. Cheaper fuel such as kerosene, paraffin, benzene and powdered coal (cheaper than petrol and diesel).
5. Used in water scarcity areas (little water).
6. Less pollution and less water is required.

- Disadvantages of gas turbine power plant:
1. 66% of power is used by compressor (low thermal efficiency).
2. The speed is (40,000 to 100,000 rpm), temperature is as high as 1100 -1260 C, so special metals and alloys have to be used for the various parts of the turbine.
3. High frequency noise from the compressor is objectionable.

- Improvement of thermal efficiency:
1. Intercooling: Compressor work is reduced by compressing the air in two stages (Intercooler in between).
2. Reheating
The output of gas turbine can be improved by expanding the gasses in two stages (reheater in between).
3. Regeneration
The high temperature of exhaust gasses is used to heat air coming from the compressor (reducing fuel supplied in the combustion chamber).


Ch.6: Wind Turbine



- Wind energy (indirect solar energy) caused by the unequal heating of the atmosphere by the sun.
- Wind Turbine: kinetic energy to mechanical energy.
- How does a Wind Turbine Work?
= Wind turns blades around a rotor (connected to the main shaft, which spins a generator).
= Mounted on tower (30 m or higher) to take advantage of faster & less turbulent wind.
= For single building or electricity grid.

- 2 Types of wind turbines:
1. Horizontal axis wind turbine (HAWT).
2. Vertical axis wind turbine (VAWT).

- Wind power system:
1. Tower.
2. Wind turbine with two or three blades.
3. Mechanical gear.
4. Electrical generator.
5. Speed sensors and control

- Parts of wind turbine (رسمة مهمة):
1. Anemometer: Measures the wind speed.
2. The nacelle: contains the key components (gearbox, generator)
3. The tower: carries the nacelle and the rotor (high tower = high wind speeds)
4. The rotor blades: most turbines have two or three blades capture wind energy and transfer its power to the rotor hub.
5. The generator: mechanical energy of the rotating shaft to electrical.
6. Controller: starts up the machine at Wind speeds of about 8 - 16 mph and shuts off the machine at about 55 mph (turbine might be damaged by high speed).
7. The gearbox: increases the rotational speed of the shaft for the generator.
8. Rotor:
The portion of the wind turbine that collects energy from the wind.
The blades are attached to the hub (attached to the main shaft).

- Rotor blade design parameters: (Length, number, pitch, shape, materials, weight).
- Even number of blades will cause stability problems.

- Operation of HAWT:
1- The kinetic energy of wind drives the turbine at low speed producing mechanical energy.
2. The gearbox: transmits mechanical energy at high speed to the generator.
3. Wind farms are located where wind has continuous, steady speed.
4. Best wind speed is 6 - 30 m/s.
5. A wind farm has small or medium size units.
6. Large units are generally single units.

- Wind energy power
K.E = 0.5 p A V³ t (joule)
Power = 0.5 p A V³ (Watt)
Mass flow rate/sec: m / t = p A V
Power = 0.5 (m/t) V²
Power density: P/A= 0.5 p V³
- Speed wind variation (رسمة مهمة)
Total wind power input > Usable power input > Actual turbine power output.
- Turbine efficiency (قوانين)

- Power curve of wind turbine:
1. Wind turbines start running at 3 to 5 m/s (cut in wind speed).
2. The cut in wind speed drive and state the amount of power loss between usable input power and actual power output.
3. Wind turbine stops at high speed (cut out Wind speed) to avoid damage.

- Factors affecting the shape of power-speed curve:
1. Rotor swept area.
2. Choice of the aero foil.
3. Number and shape of the blades.
4. Optimum tip-speed ratio.
5. Speed of rotation.
6. Aerodynamics efficiency and generator efficiency.

- Advantages of wind turbine
1. Clean (doesn't pollute environment).
2. No emissions.
3. Lowest priced renewable energy technologies.

- Disadvantages of wind turbine:
1. Wind is irregular and it does not always blow when electricity is needed.
2. High level of uncertainty in prediction.
3. Wind energy cannot be stored without batteries.
4. Good sites are located far from cities where electricity is needed.
5. The noise produced by the rotor blades.
